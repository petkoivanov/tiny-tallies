### 1.3 Number & Operations -- Fractions (4.NF)

**Critical focus area for Grade 4.** This is where the most new conceptual ground is broken.

#### Cluster A: Extend understanding of fraction equivalence and ordering

| Code | Standard | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| **4.NF.A.1** | Explain why a fraction a/b is equivalent to a fraction (n x a)/(n x b) by using visual fraction models, with attention to how the number and size of the parts differ even though the two fractions are the same size. Use this principle to recognize and generate equivalent fractions. | 1/3 = 2/6 = 3/9. Use fraction strips to show same length. |
| **4.NF.A.2** | Compare two fractions with different numerators and different denominators, e.g., by creating common denominators or numerators, or by comparing to a benchmark fraction such as 1/2. Recognize that comparisons are valid only when the two fractions refer to the same whole. Record results with symbols >, =, or <, and justify conclusions. | Compare 3/8 and 2/5: benchmark -- 3/8 < 1/2, 2/5 < 1/2. Common denominator: 15/40 vs 16/40, so 3/8 < 2/5. |

#### Cluster B: Build fractions from unit fractions

| Code | Standard | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| **4.NF.B.3** | Understand a fraction a/b with a > 1 as a sum of fractions 1/b. | 5/4 = 1/4 + 1/4 + 1/4 + 1/4 + 1/4 |
| **4.NF.B.3a** | Understand addition and subtraction of fractions as joining and separating parts referring to the same whole. | 2/5 + 1/5 means joining 2 fifths and 1 fifth = 3 fifths |
| **4.NF.B.3b** | Decompose a fraction into a sum of fractions with the same denominator in more than one way, recording each decomposition by an equation. | 3/8 = 1/8 + 1/8 + 1/8. Also: 3/8 = 1/8 + 2/8. |
| **4.NF.B.3c** | Add and subtract mixed numbers with like denominators, e.g., by replacing each mixed number with an equivalent fraction, and/or by using properties of operations and the relationship between addition and subtraction. | 2 3/4 + 1 2/4 = 3 5/4 = 4 1/4. Or: 11/4 + 6/4 = 17/4 = 4 1/4. |
| **4.NF.B.3d** | Solve word problems involving addition and subtraction of fractions referring to the same whole and having like denominators. | "Jake ate 2/6 of a pizza and Sam ate 3/6. How much did they eat in total?" => 5/6. |
| **4.NF.B.4** | Apply and extend previous understandings of multiplication to multiply a fraction by a whole number. | (parent standard) |
| **4.NF.B.4a** | Understand a fraction a/b as a multiple of 1/b. | 5/4 = 5 x (1/4) |
| **4.NF.B.4b** | Understand a multiple of a/b as a multiple of 1/b, and use this understanding to multiply a fraction by a whole number. | 3 x (2/5) = 6 x (1/5) = 6/5 = 1 1/5. |
| **4.NF.B.4c** | Solve word problems involving multiplication of a fraction by a whole number, e.g., by using visual fraction models and equations to represent the problem. | "Each serving uses 3/4 cup of flour. How much flour for 6 servings?" => 6 x 3/4 = 18/4 = 4 2/4 = 4 1/2 cups. |

#### Cluster C: Understand decimal notation for fractions, and compare decimal fractions

| Code | Standard | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| **4.NF.C.5** | Express a fraction with denominator 10 as an equivalent fraction with denominator 100, and use this technique to add two fractions with respective denominators 10 and 100. | 3/10 = 30/100. So 3/10 + 4/100 = 30/100 + 4/100 = 34/100. |
| **4.NF.C.6** | Use decimal notation for fractions with denominators 10 or 100. | 62/100 = 0.62. 7/10 = 0.7. |
| **4.NF.C.7** | Compare two decimals to hundredths by reasoning about their size. Recognize that comparisons are valid only when the two decimals refer to the same whole. Record the results with >, =, or <, and justify. | 0.36 < 0.4 because 0.4 = 0.40, and 36 hundredths < 40 hundredths. |

## 2. Specific Skills Within Each Standard

### 2.1 Operations & Algebraic Thinking -- Skills Breakdown

**4.OA.A.1 -- Multiplicative Comparison**
- Interpret "times as many" language
- Distinguish multiplicative from additive comparison ("3 more" vs "3 times as many")
- Write multiplication equations from comparison statements
- Read multiplication equations as comparison statements

**4.OA.A.2 -- Word Problems with Multiplicative Comparison**
- Solve "unknown product" problems (A has 3, B has 5 times as many, how many does B have?)
- Solve "unknown factor" problems (A has 40, which is 8 times as many as B. How many does B have?)
- Solve "unknown multiplier" problems (A has 6 and B has 30. B has how many times as many as A?)

**4.OA.A.3 -- Multi-step Word Problems**
- Identify the operations needed in multi-step problems
- Interpret remainders in context (discard, round up, or use as the answer)
- Use estimation to check reasonableness
- Use a letter for the unknown quantity

**4.OA.B.4 -- Factors and Multiples**
- List all factor pairs of a number systematically
- Recognize that 1 is a factor of every number
- Identify a number as prime or composite
- Determine if one number is a multiple of another
- Use divisibility rules (2, 3, 5, 9, 10)

**4.OA.C.5 -- Patterns**
- Generate patterns from a rule (e.g., start at 2, multiply by 3)
- Identify features not stated in the rule (always even, always increasing, alternating)
- Describe patterns using mathematical language

### 2.2 Number & Operations in Base Ten -- Skills Breakdown

**4.NBT.A.1 -- Place Value Understanding (10x relationship)**
- Explain that each place is 10x the place to its right
- Compare digits in different places within the same number
- Apply this understanding to numbers up to 1,000,000

**4.NBT.A.2 -- Read, Write, Compare Multi-digit Numbers**
- Read numbers to 1,000,000 in standard, word, and expanded form
- Write numbers in all three forms
- Compare using >, <, = based on place value

**4.NBT.A.3 -- Round Multi-digit Numbers**
- Round to any place (tens, hundreds, thousands, ten-thousands, etc.)
- Apply rounding in context (estimation)
- Understand rounding as finding the nearest multiple

**4.NBT.B.4 -- Multi-digit Addition and Subtraction (Fluency)**
- Add numbers with up to 6 digits with regrouping
- Subtract numbers with up to 6 digits with borrowing
- Subtract across zeros (e.g., 40,000 - 27,683)

**4.NBT.B.5 -- Multi-digit Multiplication**
- Multiply up to 4-digit by 1-digit (e.g., 4,326 x 7)
- Multiply 2-digit by 2-digit (e.g., 43 x 28)
- Use area model / partial products
- Use the standard algorithm (developing fluency, not required to be fluent)
- Estimate products using rounding

**4.NBT.B.6 -- Division with Remainders**
- Divide up to 4-digit by 1-digit (e.g., 7,842 / 6)
- Find quotients and remainders
- Use partial quotients strategy
- Use the relationship between multiplication and division to check
- Interpret remainders in context

### 2.3 Fractions -- Skills Breakdown

**4.NF.A.1 -- Equivalent Fractions**
- Use visual models (fraction strips, number lines, area models) to show equivalence
- Generate equivalent fractions by multiplying numerator and denominator by the same number
- Simplify fractions (find equivalent fraction with smaller numbers)
- Recognize that a/a = 1 for any non-zero a

**4.NF.A.2 -- Compare Fractions**
- Compare using common denominators
- Compare using common numerators
- Compare using benchmark fractions (0, 1/2, 1)
- Compare fractions with same denominator (compare numerators)
- Compare fractions with same numerator (compare denominators -- larger denominator = smaller fraction)

**4.NF.B.3 -- Add/Subtract Fractions with Like Denominators**
- Add fractions with like denominators
- Subtract fractions with like denominators
- Add mixed numbers with like denominators
- Subtract mixed numbers with like denominators (including regrouping)
- Decompose fractions in multiple ways (e.g., 7/8 = 3/8 + 4/8 = 5/8 + 2/8)

**4.NF.B.4 -- Multiply Fractions by Whole Numbers**
- Understand a/b as a x (1/b)
- Multiply a whole number by a unit fraction (4 x 1/3 = 4/3)
- Multiply a whole number by a non-unit fraction (3 x 2/5 = 6/5)
- Convert improper fractions to mixed numbers
- Solve word problems with fraction x whole number

**4.NF.C.5-7 -- Decimal Fractions**
- Convert tenths to hundredths (3/10 = 30/100)
- Write fractions as decimals (7/10 = 0.7, 43/100 = 0.43)
- Write decimals as fractions
- Compare decimals to hundredths
- Add fractions with denominators 10 and 100 using equivalence
- Locate decimals on a number line

### 2.4 Measurement & Data -- Skills Breakdown

**4.MD.A.1 -- Unit Conversions**
- Convert within metric system (km/m/cm, kg/g, L/mL)
- Convert within customary system (ft/in, lb/oz, gal/qt/pt/cup)
- Convert time units (hr/min/sec, days/hr)
- Create and use two-column conversion tables

**4.MD.A.2 -- Measurement Word Problems**
- Solve problems involving distances
- Solve problems involving time intervals
- Solve problems involving liquid volumes and masses
- Solve problems involving money with decimals

**4.MD.A.3 -- Area and Perimeter**
- Apply area formula: A = l x w
- Apply perimeter formula: P = 2l + 2w or P = 2(l + w)
- Find missing dimensions given area or perimeter
- Distinguish between area and perimeter in context

**4.MD.B.4 -- Line Plots with Fractional Data**
- Create line plots with measurements in halves, quarters, eighths
- Read and interpret line plots
- Solve addition/subtraction problems using line plot data

**4.MD.C.5-7 -- Angles**
- Identify angles in real-world objects
- Classify angles as acute, right, obtuse, straight, reflex
- Use a protractor to measure angles
- Draw angles of specified measure
- Solve for unknown angles using angle addition

### 2.5 Geometry -- Skills Breakdown

**4.G.A.1 -- Lines and Angles**
- Draw and identify points, lines, line segments, rays
- Identify and draw right, acute, and obtuse angles
- Identify and draw parallel and perpendicular lines
- Find these elements in real-world figures

**4.G.A.2 -- Classify 2D Figures**
- Classify triangles by angles (right, acute, obtuse)
- Classify quadrilaterals by parallel/perpendicular sides
- Understand hierarchy: square is a special rectangle, rectangle is a special parallelogram
- Identify right triangles

**4.G.A.3 -- Symmetry**
- Identify lines of symmetry in 2D figures
- Draw lines of symmetry
- Determine how many lines of symmetry a figure has
- Create symmetric figures

## 4. Difficulty Progression Within Grade 4

### 4.1 Recommended Teaching Sequence

Based on Common Core progression documents, Fishtank Learning curriculum, and Bridges Math:

**Phase 1: Place Value and Whole Number Operations (Weeks 1-8)**
1. Place value understanding to 1,000,000 (4.NBT.A.1-3) -- foundational
2. Multi-digit addition and subtraction fluency (4.NBT.B.4) -- consolidates Grade 3
3. Multiplicative comparison concepts (4.OA.A.1-2) -- new concept, relies on multiplication facts
4. Multi-step word problems (4.OA.A.3) -- applies all four operations

**Phase 2: Multiplication and Division (Weeks 9-16)**
5. Multi-digit multiplication: 1-digit x up to 4-digit (4.NBT.B.5) -- builds on place value
6. Multi-digit multiplication: 2-digit x 2-digit (4.NBT.B.5) -- extends with area model
7. Division with remainders (4.NBT.B.6) -- uses multiplication relationship
8. Factors, multiples, primes (4.OA.B.4) -- requires strong multiplication/division

**Phase 3: Fraction Concepts and Operations (Weeks 17-26)**
9. Fraction equivalence (4.NF.A.1) -- builds on Grade 3 fraction foundations
10. Fraction comparison (4.NF.A.2) -- uses equivalence
11. Add/subtract fractions with like denominators (4.NF.B.3a-b) -- uses fraction understanding
12. Add/subtract mixed numbers (4.NF.B.3c-d) -- extends fraction add/subtract
13. Multiply fractions by whole numbers (4.NF.B.4) -- bridges fractions and multiplication

**Phase 4: Decimals (Weeks 27-30)**
14. Tenths and hundredths as fractions and decimals (4.NF.C.5-6) -- builds on fraction equivalence
15. Compare decimals (4.NF.C.7) -- uses place value understanding

**Phase 5: Measurement, Data, and Geometry (Weeks 31-36)**
16. Unit conversions (4.MD.A.1-2) -- uses multiplication/division
17. Area and perimeter formulas (4.MD.A.3) -- uses multiplication
18. Line plots with fractional data (4.MD.B.4) -- uses fraction operations
19. Angles: concepts, measurement, addition (4.MD.C.5-7) -- new conceptual domain
20. Lines, rays, angles, shapes classification (4.G.A.1-2) -- uses angle concepts
21. Line symmetry (4.G.A.3) -- spatial reasoning
22. Patterns (4.OA.C.5) -- integrates throughout or taught late

## 5. Common Misconceptions at Grade 4 Level

### 5.1 Operations & Algebraic Thinking Misconceptions

| Misconception | What Students Think | Correct Understanding | Bug Library Pattern |
|---------------|--------------------|-----------------------|---------------------|
| **Additive vs. multiplicative confusion** | "4 times as many as 3" means 4 + 3 = 7 | It means 4 x 3 = 12 | Student adds instead of multiplying for "times as many" problems |
| **Remainder ignored** | 128 / 30 = 4 (drops the remainder) | Must interpret remainder: need 5 buses | Student gives quotient without considering context |
| **Remainder always reported** | 128 / 30 = 4 R8 regardless of context | Sometimes round up, sometimes remainder is the answer | Student always writes "R" format |
| **Prime/composite confusion** | 1 is prime; 2 is not prime because it is even | 1 is neither prime nor composite; 2 is the only even prime | Student marks 1 as prime, or 2 as composite |
| **Factor pair incompleteness** | Factors of 36: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 36 (misses 9, 12, 18) | Must check systematically up to sqrt(n) | Student stops checking after finding a few pairs |

### 5.2 Number & Operations in Base Ten Misconceptions

| Misconception | What Students Think | Correct Understanding | Bug Library Pattern |
|---------------|--------------------|-----------------------|---------------------|
| **"Add a zero" for x10** | 4.5 x 10 = 4.50 | 4.5 x 10 = 45 (digits shift left) | Student appends zero instead of shifting |
| **Place value as labels** | In 4,400 both 4s are the same | The 4 in thousands is 10x the 4 in hundreds | Student treats digit value as face value |
| **Subtraction across zeros** | 4,000 - 2,637: forgets to regroup across multiple zeros | Must regroup: 4,000 = 3,999 + 1, then subtract | Errors in borrowing chains |
| **Multiplication partial products error** | 34 x 27: computes 34 x 2 + 34 x 7 = 68 + 238 = 306 | 34 x 27 = 34 x 20 + 34 x 7 = 680 + 238 = 918 | Forgets the place value of tens digit |
| **Division: quotient digit placement** | 848 / 4: puts 2 over the 8 in hundreds, gets 2R48 | 848 / 4 = 212 | Misaligns quotient digits |

### 5.3 Fractions Misconceptions (Highest Error Domain)

| Misconception | What Students Think | Correct Understanding | Bug Library Pattern |
|---------------|--------------------|-----------------------|---------------------|
| **Whole-number thinking for comparison** | 1/4 > 1/2 because 4 > 2 | Larger denominator = smaller pieces, so 1/4 < 1/2 | Student compares denominators as whole numbers |
| **Add across numerators AND denominators** | 1/3 + 1/4 = 2/7 | Must find common denominator: 4/12 + 3/12 = 7/12 | Student adds both top and bottom (NOTE: Grade 4 only adds like denominators, but students attempt unlike) |
| **Multiply both by whole number** | 3 x 2/5: multiplies both num and denom = 6/15 | Only multiply the numerator: 3 x 2/5 = 6/5 | Student multiplies denominator by whole number too |
| **Equivalent fractions: add instead of multiply** | 1/3 = 2/4 (added 1 to both) | 1/3 = 2/6 (multiplied both by 2) | Student adds same value to num and denom |
| **Mixed number addition without regrouping** | 2 3/4 + 1 3/4 = 3 6/4 (leaves as is) | 3 6/4 = 4 2/4 = 4 1/2 | Student does not convert improper fraction part |
| **Fractions are not numbers** | Fractions are "parts of things," not quantities on a number line | Fractions are numbers that can be placed on a number line | Student cannot locate fractions on number line |
| **Larger fraction = more pieces** | 3/8 > 2/4 because 3 > 2 and 8 > 4 | Must compare using common denominators or benchmarks | Student compares numerators and denominators separately |

### 5.4 Decimal Misconceptions

| Misconception | What Students Think | Correct Understanding | Bug Library Pattern |
|---------------|--------------------|-----------------------|---------------------|
| **Longer decimal = larger number** | 0.36 > 0.4 because 36 > 4 | 0.4 = 0.40, so 0.40 > 0.36 | Student applies whole-number comparison to decimals |
| **Decimal as two separate whole numbers** | 3.14 is "3 and 14" | 3.14 is 3 ones, 1 tenth, 4 hundredths | Student treats parts before and after decimal point independently |
| **Tenths and hundredths confusion** | 0.5 = 0.05 | 0.5 = 0.50, which is 10x larger than 0.05 | Student confuses tenths and hundredths places |

### 5.5 Measurement & Data Misconceptions

| Misconception | What Students Think | Correct Understanding | Bug Library Pattern |
|---------------|--------------------|-----------------------|---------------------|
| **Conversion direction error** | 5 km = 5 / 1000 = 0.005 m | 5 km = 5 x 1000 = 5,000 m (larger unit to smaller = multiply) | Student divides when should multiply (or vice versa) |
| **Area vs. perimeter confusion** | Area of 4 x 6 rectangle = 20 (computed perimeter) | Area = 24 sq units, Perimeter = 20 units | Student computes wrong measure |
| **Same perimeter = same area** | Two rectangles with P=20 must have same area | 4x6 has A=24, 2x8 has A=16, both have P=20 | Student assumes perimeter determines area |

### 5.6 Geometry Misconceptions

| Misconception | What Students Think | Correct Understanding | Bug Library Pattern |
|---------------|--------------------|-----------------------|---------------------|
| **Longer rays = bigger angle** | An acute angle with long rays is bigger than an obtuse angle with short rays | Angle size depends on rotation, not ray length | Student judges angle by ray length |
| **Angles must be oriented "standard" way** | An angle opening to the left or downward is not recognized | Angles can open in any direction | Student only identifies angles opening rightward/upward |
| **Square is not a rectangle** | Squares and rectangles are different things | A square is a special rectangle (4 right angles, opposite sides parallel and equal) | Student rejects "square" when asked to identify rectangles |
| **Symmetry only vertical** | Only vertical lines can be lines of symmetry | Lines of symmetry can be vertical, horizontal, diagonal | Student only identifies vertical symmetry lines |

## 6. International Curriculum Comparison

### 6.1 Singapore Math -- Primary 4

**Source:** MOE Singapore syllabus, Singapore Math Inc. scope and sequence

**Book 4A Topics:**
1. Whole Numbers to 100,000 (place value, comparison, ordering, rounding)
2. Addition and Subtraction (multi-digit, estimation, word problems)
3. Multiplication and Division (up to 4-digit by 1-digit, 2-digit by 2-digit)
4. Factors and Multiples (factor pairs, common factors, common multiples)
5. Fractions (equivalent fractions, mixed numbers/improper fractions, add/subtract unlike denominators)
6. Angles (measuring, drawing, turns)
7. Lines and Shapes (perpendicular, parallel, quadrilateral properties)
8. Area and Perimeter (composite figures)

**Book 4B Topics:**
9. Decimals: Tenths, Hundredths, Thousandths (US does NOT cover thousandths in Grade 4)
10. Four Operations of Decimals (add, subtract, multiply, divide decimals -- US defers most to Grade 5)
11. Congruent and Symmetric Figures
12. Coordinate Graphs and Changes in Quantities
13. Data Analysis (tables, bar graphs)
14. Measures and Volume

**Key Differences from US Common Core:**
| Topic | Singapore P4 | US CCSS Grade 4 |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| Decimal operations | Add, subtract, multiply, divide decimals | Only introduce decimal notation; compare decimals |
| Thousandths | Covered | Not until Grade 5 |
| Unlike-denominator fraction add/sub | Introduced | Not until Grade 5 |
| Bar models | Signature technique, used extensively | Not required (some curricula use them) |
| Coordinate graphs | Introduced | Not until Grade 5 |
| Numbers range | To 100,000 | To 1,000,000 (larger range) |
| Volume | Covered | Not until Grade 5 |

**Singapore is approximately 6-12 months ahead of US Common Core in fractions and decimals but covers a smaller number range.**

### 6.2 Russian Math -- Grade 4 Equivalent

**Source:** TIMSS 2015 Encyclopedia (Russian Federation), RSM curriculum, Peterson method materials

**Russian Grade 4 curriculum topics (approximate, synthesized from multiple sources):**

1. **Numbers to 1,000,000** -- reading, writing, comparing, ordering
2. **Multi-digit Arithmetic** -- all four operations with multi-digit numbers, including multi-digit by multi-digit multiplication (US limits to 2-digit x 2-digit)
3. **Equations and Unknowns** -- solving for x in equations like 3x + 5 = 20 (US does not introduce formal equation-solving until Grade 6)
4. **Fractions** -- equivalent fractions, comparing, adding/subtracting with unlike denominators, multiplying fractions
5. **Decimals** -- all four operations with decimals
6. **Measurement** -- metric and non-metric, unit conversions, speed/distance/time problems
7. **Geometry** -- angles, polygons, area, perimeter, basic constructions with compass and straightedge
8. **Logic and Reasoning** -- set problems, Venn diagrams, combinatorics, logic puzzles
9. **Percentages** -- introduced (US: Grade 6)
10. **Speed/Distance/Time** -- rate problems (US: Grade 5-6)

**Key Differences from US Common Core:**
| Topic | Russian Grade 4 | US CCSS Grade 4 |
|-------|----------------|-----------------|
| Algebraic equations | Solve for unknowns formally | Use letter for unknown, but no formal solving |
| Multi-digit x multi-digit | Full multi-digit multiplication | Limited to 2-digit x 2-digit |
| Percentages | Introduced | Grade 6 |
| Speed/distance/time | Covered | Grade 5-6 |
| Logic puzzles | Core part of curriculum | Not in standards |
| Abstract reasoning | Heavy emphasis | Moderate emphasis |
| Mental math | Extensive required | Encouraged but not tested |

**Russian math is approximately 1-2 years ahead of US Common Core in algebraic thinking and problem complexity.**

### 6.3 UK National Curriculum -- Year 4 and Year 5

**Source:** GOV.UK National Curriculum Mathematics Programmes of Study

UK Year 4 students are ages 8-9 (same as US Grade 3-4 transition). UK Year 5 students are ages 9-10 (same as US Grade 4-5 transition). The combined Year 4-5 content maps roughly to US Grade 4.

**Year 4 Programme of Study:**

*Number -- Place Value:*
- Count in multiples of 6, 7, 9, 25, and 1,000
- Find 1,000 more or less
- Count backwards through zero to negative numbers
- Recognize place value in 4-digit numbers
- Round to nearest 10, 100, or 1,000
- Read Roman numerals to 100 (I to C)

*Number -- Addition and Subtraction:*
- Add and subtract up to 4 digits using formal written methods
- Estimate and use inverse operations to check

*Number -- Multiplication and Division:*
- Recall all multiplication and division facts up to 12 x 12 (US: by end of Grade 3, up to 10 x 10)
- Multiply 2-digit and 3-digit by 1-digit using formal written layout
- Recognize and use factor pairs and commutativity

*Number -- Fractions (including Decimals):*
- Recognize equivalent fractions; count in hundredths
- Add and subtract fractions with same denominator
- Recognize decimal equivalents of 1/4, 1/2, 3/4
- Find effect of dividing by 10 and 100
- Round decimals to nearest whole number

*Measurement:*
- Convert between units (km/m, hr/min, etc.)
- Measure and calculate perimeter of rectilinear figures
- Find area by counting squares
- Convert time between analogue/digital, 12/24-hour clocks

*Geometry:*
- Compare and classify shapes by properties
- Identify acute and obtuse angles
- Identify lines of symmetry
- Describe positions as coordinates in first quadrant
- Describe translations

*Statistics:*
- Interpret discrete and continuous data
- Solve comparison, sum, and difference problems from charts

**Year 5 Additions (beyond Year 4, mapping to US Grade 4-5):**
- Numbers to 1,000,000
- Negative numbers in context
- Add/subtract fractions with related denominators (multiples)
- Multiply proper fractions and mixed numbers by whole numbers
- Decimal place value to thousandths
- Percentages introduced
- Volume and capacity
- Angles: measure, draw, identify at a point and on straight line
- Prime numbers, square numbers, cube numbers

**Key Differences from US Common Core:**
| Topic | UK Year 4 | US CCSS Grade 4 |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| Times tables | Up to 12 x 12 required | Up to 10 x 10 (from Grade 3) |
| Negative numbers | Introduced (counting back through zero) | Not until Grade 6 |
| Roman numerals | Required (to 100) | Not in Common Core |
| Coordinates | First quadrant introduced | Not until Grade 5 |
| Translations | Described as movements | Not until Grade 5-8 |
| Statistics | Discrete and continuous data | Line plots only |
| Area | By counting squares | By formula (l x w) |

### 7.3 Bug Library Patterns for Grade 4

Priority misconception patterns for the misconception detection system (most impactful):

1. **Whole-number thinking in fractions** (1/4 > 1/2) -- HIGH frequency, HIGH impact
2. **Add numerators AND denominators** (1/3 + 1/4 = 2/7) -- HIGH frequency
3. **Longer decimal = larger** (0.36 > 0.4) -- HIGH frequency
4. **Additive-multiplicative confusion** (4 times as many = 4 + n) -- MEDIUM frequency
5. **Partial products place value error** (34 x 27: treats tens digit as ones) -- HIGH frequency
6. **Multiply both num and denom by whole number** (3 x 2/5 = 6/15) -- MEDIUM frequency
7. **Conversion direction error** (multiply vs divide for unit conversion) -- MEDIUM frequency
8. **Angle size by ray length** -- MEDIUM frequency
9. **Equivalent fraction by addition** (1/3 -> 2/4 by adding 1 to each) -- MEDIUM frequency
10. **Ignore/misinterpret remainders** -- MEDIUM frequency
